Integrated Human Practices

↓

Our iGEM project has been shaped by a deep integration of ethical, environmental, and social considerations through our Human Practices (iHP) work. From the outset, our goal has been to address the widespread issue of Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR) in a way that minimizes environmental harm and maximizes social benefit. Engaging with stakeholders in the coffee industry, including farmers and agricultural experts, has provided critical insights into the ecological impact of chemical treatments and the challenges farmers face in adopting sustainable alternatives. This feedback has significantly influenced our project’s design, prompting us to focus on developing a biocontrol agent that is both environmentally friendly and economically viable, ensuring that the end-users—farmers—benefit from a solution that aligns with their resources and farming practices.

Ethical considerations have played a central role in shaping the experiments and approaches we have employed in the lab. Stakeholder input highlighted the importance of ensuring that our biocontrol agent is effective and safe for the surrounding ecosystems and non-target species. We designed our experiments with these factors, prioritizing rigorous testing to ensure that our solution poses no undue environmental risk. Ethical feedback also emphasized the need for transparency and responsible scientific practice, which has led us to adopt more stringent documentation and safety protocols in our lab work. Throughout the competition, we have continuously refined our project based on ongoing stakeholder input, ensuring that every step of our research and development process remains aligned with the values and concerns of the communities we aim to serve.

This iterative approach to integrating societal and stakeholder feedback has been fundamental to the evolution of our project. As we moved through the iGEM competition, our Human Practices team remained actively engaged with stakeholders, whose insights guided crucial pivots in our project’s direction. These exchanges allowed us to validate the real-world impact of our biocontrol agent and ensure that it meets the ethical, social, and environmental criteria necessary for successful implementation. By incorporating diverse perspectives and addressing societal needs, our project has grown into a comprehensive solution that not only tackles CLR but also has the potential for broad application in sustainable agriculture, empowering communities and protecting ecosystems globally.

He recommended homology flanks between 500-1kb for transformation, as shorter ones (~350bp) yield low success rates, and offered protocols for transformations and media preparation.
